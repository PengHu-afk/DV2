The Hidden Comfort Map of Australian Road Trips

This visualisation explores whether public toilet access supports the needs of road-trip travellers across Australia. It looks beyond where toilets exist and asks whether they are useful for night drivers, families, accessible travellers and caravan users.

The question

Are public toilets available where road-trip travellers need them?

The project moves from travel demand to spatial access, then from toilet quantity to traveller readiness. The final section turns the evidence into improvement priorities.

Where demand starts

Domestic travel creates repeated pressure on roadside facilities

Before judging whether public toilets are enough, the story first identifies where domestic travel is concentrated and when tourism pressure rises across the year.

Demand is concentrated in three large states

New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland consistently dominate domestic trips across 2025. This means public toilet access is not just a remote-area issue: the busiest travel states also need reliable roadside facilities.

The line chart shows actual travel volume rather than rank, so the scale gap between states is visible.

Reading guide: higher lines indicate larger domestic travel demand. Values are shown in million domestic trips for each 2025 quarter.

Reading guide: darker cells indicate higher tourism pressure for the selected metric. Outlined cells mark each state's peak quarter.

Key insight: Travel demand is consistently concentrated in New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland, while seasonal peaks still change across quarters. Reliable roadside facilities are therefore a continuing need, not a one-off peak issue.
Where toilets are

Toilet access is spatial, not just numerical

Raw toilet counts can overemphasise cities. The next maps look at relative supply and show how the toilet network changes when filtered by traveller needs.

Reading guide: each square represents a geographic grid cell. Darker squares indicate lower relative toilet supply per million domestic trips. Blank cells indicate areas with no recorded public toilets or outside the selected grid coverage.

Reading guide: each panel filters the toilet dataset by one traveller need. Points show toilets that support that need.

Different needs create different maps

The same toilet network looks different for night drivers, accessible travellers, families and caravan users. A place can appear well supplied overall but still be weak for a specific traveller group.

Key insight: Toilet access should be evaluated by usefulness, not only location. Need-based maps reveal gaps that raw toilet counts can hide.
Demand versus supply

Large toilet networks can still face pressure

The next section compares travel demand, toilet supply and readiness. The question is not simply whether a state has many toilets, but whether those toilets match traveller demand and needs.

High supply does not always mean low pressure

States with large toilet networks can still face pressure when domestic travel demand is also high. This is why the chart uses trips per toilet as a pressure measure rather than raw toilet count alone.

Reading guide: states higher on the chart face stronger domestic travel demand. States further right have more public toilets. Darker points indicate more trips per toilet.

Reading guide: each row is one state. Brown points show toilet quantity rank, while blue points show road-trip readiness rank. Positive gaps mean readiness rank is worse than quantity rank.

Key insight: Quantity and usefulness do not always tell the same story. Road-trip readiness also depends on features such as accessibility, opening hours, parking and traveller facilities.
Who is supported

Different travellers experience the same network differently

This section shifts from infrastructure counts to user needs, asking which facility types and states support night drivers, families, accessible travellers and caravan users.

Reading guide: darker heatmap cells mean a higher share of toilets in that facility type provide the selected feature. The side bars show how common each facility type is.

Night drivers are better supported than caravan travellers

The persona matrix shows that public toilet support is uneven across traveller groups. Night drivers tend to be better supported, while caravan support is weaker and more uneven.

Reading guide: darker cells and percentage labels show stronger support for each traveller persona. Circle size is kept subtle to reduce visual overload.

Key insight: Public toilet planning should consider user groups, not only total facility counts. A network that works for one traveller may be less useful for another.
What should improve

Improvements should target weaker support features

The final section turns the analysis into a decision framework. The readiness score summarises traveller-support features, while the final matrix identifies where pressure and weaker readiness overlap.

Reading guide: each stacked bar shows the weighted contribution of each feature. The black dot marks the total readiness score.

Readiness is a mix of traveller-support features

The road-trip readiness score combines accessible access, 24-hour access, parking, baby-change facilities and showers. This makes the score more transparent than a single unexplained rating.

Reading guide: points further right have higher travel pressure, while points lower on the chart have weaker readiness. No state falls clearly into a worst-case cluster, but improvement should still be targeted.

Final takeaway: The key issue is not simply adding toilets everywhere. Planning should focus on places where travel pressure is high and traveller-support features are weaker.
