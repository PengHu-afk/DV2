The Hidden Comfort Map of Australian Road Trips

This visualisation explores whether public toilet access supports the needs of road-trip travellers across Australia. It looks beyond where toilets exist and asks whether they are useful for night drivers, families, accessible travellers and caravan users.

Module 1 · Macro trend

Domestic travel creates repeated pressure on roadside facilities

Before judging whether public toilets are enough, the story first identifies where domestic travel is concentrated and when tourism pressure rises across the year.

Demand is concentrated in a few large states

New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland consistently dominate domestic trips across 2025. This means public toilet access is not just a remote-area issue: the busiest travel states also need reliable roadside facilities.

The line chart shows actual travel volume rather than rank, making the scale gap between states visible.

Module 2 · Geographic distribution

Toilet access is spatial, not just numerical

Raw toilet counts can overemphasise cities. These maps compare relative supply and show how the toilet network changes when filtered by traveller needs.

Reading guide: darker squares indicate lower relative toilet supply per million domestic trips. Blank cells indicate areas with no recorded toilets or outside the selected grid coverage.

Reading guide: each panel filters the toilet dataset by one traveller need. Points show toilets that support that need.

Key insight: A place may appear well supplied overall, but still offer weaker support for families, accessible travellers or caravan users.
Module 3 · Relationship analysis

Large toilet networks can still face pressure

The next step compares travel demand, toilet supply and readiness. The issue is not simply whether a state has many toilets, but whether those toilets match traveller demand and needs.

Reading guide: states higher on the chart face stronger domestic travel demand. States further right have more public toilets. Darker points indicate more trips per toilet.

Reading guide: brown points show toilet quantity rank, while blue points show road-trip readiness rank. Positive gaps mean readiness rank is worse than quantity rank.

Key insight: Quantity and usefulness do not always tell the same story. Road-trip readiness also depends on accessibility, opening hours, parking and traveller facilities.
Module 4 · Feature breakdown

Facility features explain why some toilets are more useful than others

This section shifts from toilet counts to facility usefulness. The heatmap shows which facility types support different traveller needs, while the feature importance chart explains which support features carry the most weight in the readiness score.

Reading guide: darker heatmap cells mean a higher share of toilets in that facility type provide the selected feature.

Reading guide: longer bars indicate features with greater weight in the custom road-trip readiness score. Tooltips also show average availability across states.

Why do these weights matter?

The feature importance chart helps explain why some toilets are more useful than others for road-trip travellers. The readiness score does not treat every feature equally. Instead, it gives greater importance to features that make a stop broadly usable, especially for more diverse traveller groups.

Accessibility and 24-hour access receive the highest weights because they strongly shape whether a toilet can serve travellers reliably across different times and circumstances. Practical features such as parking, baby-change facilities and showers still matter, but they play a more supporting role in the overall score.

Narrative takeaway: A larger toilet network is not enough on its own. The most useful stops are the ones that combine access, convenience and support for different traveller needs.
Key insight: Accessibility and 24-hour access matter most in the readiness score, but useful road-trip support also depends on practical features such as parking, baby-change facilities and showers.
Module 5 · Segmentation and conclusion

Improvements should target weaker support features

The final section turns the analysis into a decision framework. The readiness score summarises traveller-support features, while the final matrix identifies where pressure and weaker readiness overlap.

Reading guide: each stacked bar shows the weighted contribution of each feature. The black dot marks the total readiness score.

Reading guide: points further right have higher travel pressure, while points lower on the chart have weaker readiness. No state falls clearly into a worst-case cluster, but improvement should still be targeted.

What does this mean for action?

The readiness score shows that public toilet quality is built from several support features, not only from the number of toilets available. The final matrix then connects this readiness score with travel pressure, showing where improvement may be most useful.

NSW, Victoria and Queensland remain busy but relatively prepared, while ACT stands out because its readiness score is much weaker despite moderate pressure. This means improvement should be targeted: the priority is not simply adding more toilets, but improving the support features that make toilets usable for different road-trip travellers.

The key issue is not simply adding toilets everywhere. Planning should focus on places where travel pressure is high and traveller-support features are weaker.
