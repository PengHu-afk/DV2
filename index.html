The Hidden Comfort Map of Australian Road Trips

This visualisation explores whether public toilet access supports the needs of road-trip travellers across Australia. The story focuses not only on where toilets exist, but also on whether they are useful for different travellers, including night drivers, families, accessible travellers, and caravan travellers.

Opening snapshot

Domestic travel creates demand for roadside facilities

Road trips depend on more than fuel, food and scenery. Domestic travel creates millions of trips, visitor nights and regional spending. For travellers, public toilets are part of the basic infrastructure that makes regional travel comfortable, accessible and reliable.

Opening snapshot: these figures summarise the latest December quarter domestic tourism scale. They introduce why public toilet access matters before the project moves into maps, rankings and pressure analysis.

Travel demand

1. Which states dominate domestic travel demand across 2025?

Domestic travel demand is consistently concentrated in a small group of states. The ranking remains almost unchanged across 2025, suggesting that pressure on roadside facilities is not a one-off seasonal issue. New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland remain the strongest demand centres throughout the year.

Reading guide: rank 1 means the highest domestic travel demand in that quarter. Lines show how each state's demand ranking changes over time.

Seasonal pressure

2. How does domestic travel pressure change across the year?

Tourism pressure is not only different between states; it also changes across the year. This heatmap shows how each state performs across the four quarters of 2025. The dropdown allows the reader to compare domestic trips, visitor nights and regional expenditure.

Reading guide: darker cells indicate higher tourism pressure for the selected metric. This helps identify where public toilets may face stronger seasonal demand.

Spatial access

3. Where are public toilets concentrated across Australia?

Public toilet access is spatial. Instead of showing every toilet as a separate point, this bin map groups toilets into small longitude-latitude cells. Darker cells indicate places where more public toilets are concentrated. This makes dense city clusters and sparse regional areas easier to compare.

Reading guide: each square represents a geographic grid cell. Darker squares contain more public toilets. This is a density-style map, so it focuses on spatial coverage rather than individual toilet locations.

Traveller needs

4. Do different travellers see the same toilet network?

Public toilets do not support all travellers equally. These small multiple maps separate toilets by four road-trip needs: 24-hour access, accessible access, family support, and caravan support. The pattern shows that the same national toilet network can look very different depending on what travellers need.

Reading guide: each panel shows toilets that meet one traveller need. Dense clusters suggest stronger access, while sparse areas suggest that travellers may need to plan stops more carefully.

Demand and supply

5. Does public toilet supply match domestic travel demand?

This quadrant chart compares each state's domestic travel demand with its public toilet supply. The colour shows pressure, measured as trips per toilet. A state with many toilets may still face pressure if domestic travel demand is also high.

Reading guide: the dashed lines show the median values. States higher on the chart have stronger domestic travel demand. States further right have more public toilets. Darker bubbles indicate more trips per toilet, meaning greater pressure on the toilet network.

Quantity versus quality

6. Does having more toilets mean being more road-trip ready?

Toilet quantity and toilet usefulness are not the same thing. This dumbbell chart compares each state's rank for total public toilet count with its rank for road-trip readiness. A large gap suggests that a state may have many toilets but weaker traveller-support features, or fewer toilets but stronger quality.

Reading guide: each row is one state. Brown points show toilet quantity rank, while blue points show road-trip readiness rank. Rank 1 means highest. The number on the right shows the rank gap; positive values mean the readiness rank is worse than the quantity rank.

Facility usefulness

7. Which facility types actually support road-trip needs?

Not all public toilets provide the same level of support for road-trip travellers. This compound matrix compares facility types against common traveller needs such as accessibility, parking, showers, drinking water and 24-hour access. The side bars also show how common each facility type is, so the chart compares both usefulness and scale.

Reading guide: darker heatmap cells mean a higher share of toilets in that facility type provide the selected feature. The top bars summarise average availability by traveller need, while the right-side bars show how many toilets belong to each facility type.

Traveller personas

8. Does the toilet network work equally well for different travellers?

A public toilet network can look strong overall, but still feel weak for specific travellers. This glyph matrix compares support for night drivers, families, accessible travellers and caravan travellers across states. The goal is to shift the analysis from simple toilet counts to real user needs.

Reading guide: larger and darker circles show stronger support for that traveller persona in that state. The top bars summarise average support by state, while the right-side bars show which traveller personas are generally better or worse supported.

Readiness score

9. What makes a state road-trip ready?

The road-trip readiness score is a custom index created for this visualisation. It combines five traveller-support features: accessible toilets, 24-hour access, parking, baby-change facilities and showers. This component chart shows both the total score and how each feature contributes to it.

Reading guide: each stacked bar shows the weighted contribution of each feature to the final readiness score. The black dot marks the total score. This score is not an official rating; it is a simple index designed to compare road-trip toilet support across states.

Action priority

10. Where should public toilet improvements be prioritised?

The final decision matrix combines tourism pressure and road-trip toilet readiness. States on the right have more domestic trips per toilet, while states lower on the chart have weaker readiness scores. The priority is not simply to add more toilets everywhere, but to identify where high travel pressure meets weaker traveller-support facilities.

Reading guide: the dashed lines show median values. The lower-right quadrant is the most important priority area because it combines higher travel pressure with weaker road-trip readiness. Bubble size shows the size of the public toilet network.
